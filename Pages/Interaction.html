Interaction

For every action, there is an equal and opposite reaction.

LAW OF Interaction

We've already learned about Newton's First Law (Inertia, or why things resist changing their motion) and Newton's Second Law F = ma, or how forces cause acceleration). Now, we come to the third and final piece of Newton's puzzle: the idea that forces are never alone. Whenever one object exerts a force on another, the second object always exerts a force back on the first. This is the essence of Newton's Third Law of Motion.


Think about it: you can't push something without being pushed back, even if you don't always feel it strongly. This law describes the fundamental nature of how objects interact with each other.

The Law of Interaction

Newton's Third Law states: For every action, there is an equal and opposite reaction.

What does the statement mean?

The statement means that in every interaction, there is a pair of forces acting on the two interacting objects. The size of the forces on the first object equals the size of the force on the second object. The direction of the force on the first object is opposite to the direction of the force on the second object. Forces always come in pairs - equal and opposite action-reaction force pairs.

Key Concept

Forces occur in pairs: You never have a single, isolated force. Forces always result from the interaction between two objects.

Equal in magntude: The strength of the force exerted by the first object on the second is exactly the same as the strength of the force exerted by the second object on the first.

Opposite in direction: The two forces act in exactly opposite directions

Example

Newton's Third Law explains so much of what we experience:


1. Walking:

Action: Your foot pushes backward on the ground.

Reaction: The ground pushes forward on your foot, propelling you forward.


Rocket Propulsion:

Action: The rocket pushes hot exhaust gases downward.

Reaction: The hot exhaust gases push the rocket upward, launching it.


3. Swimming:

Action: A swimmer pushes water backward

Reaction:The water pushes the swimmer forward.


Recoil of a Gun

Action: The gun exerts a force forward on the bullet

Reaction:The bullet exerts an equal and opposite force backward on the gun, causing it to recoil.


Action: ownward.A bird's wings push air

Reaction: The air pushes the bird's wings upward, providing lift.


Suggested Activity

Balloon Rocket

Objective: To observe how action and reaction forces work using a balloon.

Materials:

1. 1 long balloon
2. 1 straw
3. 1–2 meters of string (or thread)
4. Tape
5. Two chairs or any supports to tie the string

Instructions:

1. Tie one end of the string to a chair or doorknob. 2. Slide the straw onto the string. 3. Tie the other end of the string to another chair, pulling it tight so the string is level. 4. Blow up the balloon without tying it, then hold the neck closed. 5. Tape the balloon to the straw without blocking the opening. 6. Let go of the balloon and watch it move along the string.

What Happened:

When the air rushes out of the balloon, it pushes backward. In response, the balloon moves forward—this is Newton’s Third Law, which states that for every action, there is an equal and opposite reaction. The air and balloon push on each other with equal force but in opposite directions.
