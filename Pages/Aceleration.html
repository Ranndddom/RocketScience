ACCELERATION

The acceleration of an object is directly proportional to the net force applied to it and inversely proportional to its mass.

LAW OF Acceleration

In our previous lesson, we learned about Newton's First Law of Motion, the Law of Inertia, which tells us what happens to an object when the net force on it is zero (it stays at rest or continues with constant velocity). But what happens when there is an unbalanced external force acting on an object? How does its motion change? This is precisely what Newton's Second Law of Motion explains


Newton's Second Law is the most quantitative of his three laws and is fundamental to understanding how forces cause motion to change.

Law Of Acceleration

The second law or law of acceleration states that: the acceleration of an object is dependent upon two variables - the net force acting upon the object and the mass of the object. The acceleration of an object depends directly upon the net force acting upon the object, and inversely upon the mass of the object.

What does the statement mean?

This means that as the force acting upon an object is increased, the acceleration of the object is increased. Likewise, as the mass of an object is increased, the acceleration of the object is decreased.

The Formula

F = ma

F is the force measured in Newtons(N)

m is the mass of the object measured in kilograms(kg)

a is the acceleration of the object measured in meters per second squared (m/s**2)

Understanding the Relationships

1. Force and Acceleration are Directly Proportional: When the mass of an object is constant, a larger net force applied to it results in a greater acceleration. The acceleration occurs in the same direction as the net force. (e.g., pushing a shopping cart harder makes it accelerate faster).


1. Mass and Acceleration are Inversely Proportional: When the same net force is applied to different objects, the object with more mass will experience less acceleration, while the object with less mass will experience greater acceleration. (e.g., pushing a toy car and a real car with the same force will cause the toy car to accelerate much more).

Example

1. Pushing a Sled: An empty sled accelerates easily; a full one is harder to accelerate because its greater mass requires more force for the same acceleration, or it will accelerate less with the same force


2. Throwing a Baseball vs. Bowling Ball: With the same throwing force, a baseball accelerates much faster than a bowling ball due to its significantly smaller mass.


3. Car Engine and Acceleration: A powerful car engine generates a large force for high acceleration. However, a heavier car accelerates less than a lighter one with the same engine power, which is why sports cars prioritize lightweight design.

Mathematical Example

Let's use the formula F = ma to solve some simple problem


Example 1: Finding Acceleration


A net force of 50 Newtons (N) is applied to a 10 kg object. What is the acceleration of the object?


Given:

F = 50 N
m = 10 kg


Formula: F = ma


Rearrange for acceleration: a = F/m


Calculate: a = 50 N / 10 kg






Example 2: Finding Force


What net force is required to accelerate a 2 kg object at a rate of 3 m/s²?


Given:

m = 2 kg
a = 3 m/s²


Formula: F = ma


Calculate: F = (2 kg)(3 m/s²)






Example 3: Finding Mass


The ball accelerates 5 m/s² when an 20 N Force was applied. What is the mass of the ball?


Given:

a = 5 m/s²
F = 20 N


Formula: F = F/a


Calculate: F = 20 m/s² / 5 N




Suggested Activity

Push the Objects!

Objective: To observe how heavier objects are harder to push, showing that more mass needs more force to move (Newton's Second Law: F = m × a).

Materials:

1. 1 empty plastic bottle
2. 1 plastic bottle filled with water or sand
3. Flat surface (floor or table)
4. Your hand!

Instructions:

1. Place the empty bottle on the floor or table.
2. Gently push it with one finger. See how easily and quickly it moves.
3. Now place the filled bottle in the same spot.
4. Try to push it the same way, with the same force.
5. Observe the difference.

What Happened:

The empty bottle moves quickly and easily. while the filled bottle moves slower or takes more effort to push because Heavier objects (more mass) need more force to move or speed up.
